# Format: Question | Category | Detailed Answer

What is DNA? | basics | DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid) is the hereditary material in humans and almost all other organisms. It contains the biological instructions that make each species unique and is composed of four chemical bases: adenine (A), guanine (G), cytosine (C), and thymine (T).
Explain gene expression | expression | Gene expression is the process by which information from a gene is used to synthesize a functional gene product, typically proteins or functional RNA molecules. It involves two main stages: transcription (DNA to RNA) and translation (RNA to protein).
What are chromosomes? | structures | Chromosomes are thread-like structures located inside the nucleus of animal and plant cells, made of protein and a single molecule of DNA. Humans have 23 pairs of chromosomes that carry genetic information in the form of genes.
How does DNA replication work? | processes | DNA replication is the biological process of producing two identical replicas of DNA from one original DNA molecule. It occurs during cell division and involves enzymes like DNA polymerase that unwind and copy the DNA strands.
What is RNA? | basics | RNA (Ribonucleic Acid) is a nucleic acid present in all living cells that acts as a messenger carrying instructions from DNA for controlling protein synthesis. Unlike DNA, RNA is usually single-stranded and contains uracil (U) instead of thymine.
Explain Mendelian inheritance | inheritance | Mendelian inheritance refers to patterns of inheritance for traits controlled by single genes with two alleles, following Mendel's laws of segregation and independent assortment. These patterns include dominant, recessive, and co-dominant inheritance.
What are mutations? | mutations | Mutations are changes in the DNA sequence that can affect gene function. They can be caused by errors during DNA replication, environmental factors, or chemical exposure. Mutations can be beneficial, harmful, or neutral to an organism.
How do genes affect traits? | traits | Genes affect traits by providing instructions for making proteins that determine physical characteristics and biological functions. Traits are usually influenced by multiple genes (polygenic inheritance) and environmental factors through complex interactions.
What is genetic engineering? | engineering | Genetic engineering is the direct manipulation of an organism's genes using biotechnology to alter its characteristics. Techniques include recombinant DNA technology, gene editing with CRISPR-Cas9, and transgenic organism creation.
Explain CRISPR technology | technology | CRISPR (Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats) is a revolutionary gene-editing technology that allows precise modification of DNA sequences in living organisms. It uses the Cas9 enzyme guided by RNA to cut and edit specific DNA sequences.
What is genetic variation? | variation | Genetic variation refers to differences in DNA sequences among individuals within a population. It arises from mutations, genetic recombination during meiosis, and gene flow between populations, and is essential for evolution and adaptation.
How does transcription work? | processes | Transcription is the first step of gene expression where DNA is copied into messenger RNA (mRNA) by the enzyme RNA polymerase. The process includes initiation, elongation, and termination phases, resulting in an RNA molecule complementary to the DNA template.
What are alleles? | genetics | Alleles are different versions of the same gene that arise by mutation and are found at the same locus (position) on chromosomes. For example, the gene for eye color may have alleles for blue, brown, or green eyes.
Explain protein synthesis | processes | Protein synthesis is the process by which cells build proteins based on genetic instructions in DNA. It involves transcription (DNA to mRNA in nucleus) and translation (mRNA to protein at ribosomes), with tRNA bringing amino acids to build polypeptide chains.
What is a genome? | basics | A genome is the complete set of genetic material (DNA or RNA) in an organism. It includes all genes and non-coding sequences. The human genome contains approximately 3 billion base pairs and 20,000-25,000 protein-coding genes.
How do cells divide? | cell_biology | Cells divide through mitosis (for growth and repair) and meiosis (for gamete production). Mitosis produces two identical diploid cells, while meiosis produces four genetically unique haploid cells through two rounds of division.
What is genetic counseling? | applications | Genetic counseling is the process of helping people understand and adapt to medical, psychological, and familial implications of genetic contributions to disease. It involves risk assessment, education, and support for decision-making about genetic testing.
Explain epigenetics | epigenetics | Epigenetics studies heritable changes in gene expression that do not involve changes to the underlying DNA sequence. Mechanisms include DNA methylation, histone modification, and non-coding RNA, which can be influenced by environment and lifestyle.
What are genetic disorders? | disorders | Genetic disorders are diseases caused by abnormalities in an individual's DNA, which can be inherited or occur spontaneously. Examples include cystic fibrosis (autosomal recessive), Huntington's disease (autosomal dominant), and Down syndrome (chromosomal).
How does DNA sequencing work? | technology | DNA sequencing is the process of determining the precise order of nucleotides within a DNA molecule. Modern methods like Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS) allow rapid, high-throughput sequencing of entire genomes or specific gene regions.
What is a gene? | basics | A gene is the basic physical and functional unit of heredity. It consists of a specific DNA sequence that codes for a protein or functional RNA molecule. Genes are arranged linearly on chromosomes and occupy specific positions called loci.
Explain DNA structure | structures | DNA has a double-helix structure composed of two complementary strands running in opposite directions. The backbone is made of sugar-phosphate groups, while the rungs consist of paired bases: A with T, and C with G, held by hydrogen bonds.
What is genetic drift? | evolution | Genetic drift is the random change in allele frequencies within a population over generations. It has a stronger effect in small populations and can lead to loss of genetic variation or fixation of alleles regardless of their fitness.
How are traits inherited? | inheritance | Traits are inherited through the transmission of genes from parents to offspring. Most traits follow Mendelian patterns (single gene), while complex traits involve multiple genes (polygenic inheritance) and environmental interactions.
What is a phenotype? | genetics | A phenotype is the observable physical or biochemical characteristics of an organism, determined by both genetic makeup (genotype) and environmental influences. Examples include height, eye color, blood type, and disease susceptibility.
Explain natural selection | evolution | Natural selection is the process by which organisms better adapted to their environment tend to survive and reproduce more successfully. It acts on phenotypic variation within populations, leading to evolutionary change over generations.
What is cloning? | technology | Cloning is the process of producing genetically identical individuals of an organism. Techniques include somatic cell nuclear transfer (used for Dolly the sheep) and molecular cloning of DNA fragments for research purposes.
How do sex chromosomes work? | inheritance | Sex chromosomes determine biological sex in many organisms. Humans have X and Y chromosomes: females are XX, males are XY. The SRY gene on the Y chromosome triggers male development, while X chromosomes carry many important genes.
What is genetic testing? | applications | Genetic testing involves analyzing DNA to identify changes in genes, chromosomes, or proteins. It can be used for diagnostic purposes, carrier screening, prenatal testing, pharmacogenomics, and ancestry analysis.
Explain DNA repair mechanisms | processes | DNA repair mechanisms correct damage to DNA molecules caused by environmental factors or replication errors. Systems include base excision repair, nucleotide excision repair, mismatch repair, and double-strand break repair using homologous recombination.
What is a codon? | genetics | A codon is a sequence of three nucleotides in DNA or RNA that specifies a particular amino acid or stop signal during protein synthesis. The genetic code consists of 64 codons, with most amino acids encoded by multiple codons (degeneracy).
How does mitochondrial DNA differ? | structures | Mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) is circular, inherited maternally, lacks histones and introns, has a higher mutation rate, and encodes 37 genes for oxidative phosphorylation. Unlike nuclear DNA, it doesn't recombine during reproduction.
What is population genetics? | applications | Population genetics studies genetic variation within and between populations over time. It examines allele frequency changes due to evolutionary forces: mutation, selection, genetic drift, gene flow, and non-random mating.
Explain genetic linkage | inheritance | Genetic linkage occurs when genes located close together on the same chromosome tend to be inherited together. The closer the genes, the lower the probability of recombination between them during meiosis.
What is RNA interference? | expression | RNA interference (RNAi) is a biological process where RNA molecules inhibit gene expression by neutralizing targeted mRNA molecules. It plays roles in defense against viruses, gene regulation, and has applications in research and therapy.
How does PCR work? | technology | Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) amplifies specific DNA sequences exponentially. It involves cycles of denaturation (heating to separate strands), annealing (cooling for primer binding), and extension (DNA synthesis by polymerase).
What is a transgenic organism? | engineering | A transgenic organism contains genetic material from another species introduced through genetic engineering. Examples include Bt cotton (with bacterial genes for pest resistance) and golden rice (with genes for beta-carotene production).
Explain Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium | evolution | The Hardy-Weinberg principle states that allele and genotype frequencies remain constant in a population from generation to generation in the absence of evolutionary forces. It serves as a null model for detecting evolutionary change.
What is gene therapy? | applications | Gene therapy involves introducing, removing, or altering genetic material within cells to treat or prevent disease. Approaches include replacing mutated genes, inactivating malfunctioning genes, or introducing new genes to fight disease.
How do stem cells work? | cell_biology | Stem cells are undifferentiated cells capable of self-renewal and differentiation into specialized cell types. Embryonic stem cells are pluripotent, while adult stem cells are multipotent and found in specific tissues for repair.
What is a karyotype? | structures | A karyotype is the number and visual appearance of chromosomes in the cell nucleus, organized in pairs and arranged by size. It's used to identify chromosomal abnormalities like trisomies, monosomies, and structural rearrangements.
Explain genomic imprinting | epigenetics | Genomic imprinting is an epigenetic phenomenon where genes are expressed differently depending on whether they are inherited from the mother or father. It involves DNA methylation and histone modifications that silence one allele.
What is a genetic marker? | applications | Genetic markers are DNA sequences with known locations on chromosomes that can be used to identify individuals or species. Types include RFLPs, microsatellites, SNPs, and are used in linkage analysis, forensics, and ancestry testing.
How does alternative splicing work? | expression | Alternative splicing allows a single gene to produce multiple protein variants by including or excluding different exons from the final mRNA. This greatly increases proteomic diversity from a limited number of genes.
What is synthetic biology? | engineering | Synthetic biology combines biology and engineering to design and construct new biological parts, devices, and systems, or to redesign existing natural biological systems for useful purposes, like biofuels or novel therapeutics.
Explain founder effect | evolution | Founder effect occurs when a new colony is started by a few members of the original population, resulting in reduced genetic variation and non-representative allele frequencies compared to the source population.
What is pharmacogenomics? | applications | Pharmacogenomics studies how genes affect individual responses to drugs. It aims to develop personalized medicine by optimizing drug efficacy and minimizing adverse reactions based on genetic makeup.
How does RNA editing work? | expression | RNA editing alters RNA sequences after transcription through chemical modifications. Common types include A-to-I editing by ADAR enzymes and C-to-U editing by APOBEC enzymes, expanding proteomic diversity.
What is a knockout mouse? | research | A knockout mouse is genetically engineered to have one or more genes made inoperative (knocked out) to study gene function. These models help understand gene roles in development, disease, and potential therapeutic targets.
Explain convergent evolution | evolution | Convergent evolution occurs when unrelated species independently evolve similar traits as adaptations to similar environments or ecological niches. Examples include wings in birds, bats, and insects, or streamlined bodies in aquatic animals.
What is metagenomics? | research | Metagenomics studies genetic material recovered directly from environmental samples, bypassing the need for isolating and culturing individual species. It's used to study microbial communities in soil, water, and human microbiome.
How does epigenetics affect health? | epigenetics | Epigenetic changes can influence health by regulating gene expression in response to environmental factors. Lifestyle, diet, stress, and toxins can cause epigenetic modifications linked to cancer, diabetes, and neurological disorders.
What is comparative genomics? | research | Comparative genomics compares genome sequences across different species to identify similarities, differences, evolutionary relationships, and functionally important regions. It helps understand gene function and evolutionary history.
Explain horizontal gene transfer | evolution | Horizontal gene transfer is the movement of genetic material between organisms other than by vertical transmission (parent to offspring). Common in bacteria through conjugation, transformation, and transduction, it spreads antibiotic resistance.
What is telomere biology? | cell_biology | Telomeres are repetitive nucleotide sequences at chromosome ends that protect against deterioration. They shorten with each cell division; telomerase enzyme maintains telomere length in stem cells and cancer cells.
How do oncogenes work? | disorders | Oncogenes are mutated forms of normal genes (proto-oncogenes) that promote uncontrolled cell growth and division. They can be activated by point mutations, gene amplification, or chromosomal translocations, leading to cancer.
What is a genetic algorithm? | applications | Genetic algorithms are optimization techniques inspired by natural selection. They use operations like selection, crossover, and mutation on a population of candidate solutions to evolve toward better solutions to computational problems.
Explain molecular phylogenetics | research | Molecular phylogenetics uses molecular data (DNA, protein sequences) to infer evolutionary relationships among organisms. It constructs phylogenetic trees showing patterns of divergence and common ancestry over time.
What is gene duplication? | evolution | Gene duplication creates extra copies of genes that can evolve new functions (neofunctionalization) or divide original functions (subfunctionalization). It's a major source of genetic novelty and complexity in genomes.
How does CRISPR diagnostics work? | technology | CRISPR diagnostics (like SHERLOCK and DETECTR) use CRISPR enzymes to detect specific nucleic acid sequences with high sensitivity. They can identify pathogens, genetic mutations, or biomarkers for disease diagnosis.
What is a punnett square? | inheritance | A Punnett square is a diagram used to predict genotype and phenotype probabilities of offspring from a genetic cross. It shows possible combinations of parental alleles and their statistical likelihood in progeny.
Explain RNA world hypothesis | evolution | The RNA world hypothesis proposes that early life used RNA for both genetic information storage and catalysis before DNA and proteins evolved. Ribozymes (catalytic RNA) and ribosomes support this hypothesis.
What is gene regulation? | expression | Gene regulation controls when, where, and how much genes are expressed. Mechanisms include transcription factors, enhancers, silencers, chromatin remodeling, and post-transcriptional modifications that respond to cellular signals.
How does meiosis create diversity? | cell_biology | Meiosis creates genetic diversity through independent assortment of chromosomes, crossing over (recombination) between homologous chromosomes, and random fertilization, producing genetically unique gametes.
What is a plasmid? | structures | Plasmids are small, circular, extrachromosomal DNA molecules in bacteria that replicate independently. They often carry genes for antibiotic resistance or other beneficial traits and are used as vectors in genetic engineering.
Explain genetic assimilation | evolution | Genetic assimilation occurs when a phenotype initially produced in response to an environmental stimulus becomes genetically fixed through natural selection, so it develops even without the original environmental trigger.
What is optogenetics? | technology | Optogenetics uses light to control cells (usually neurons) genetically modified to express light-sensitive ion channels. It allows precise manipulation of neural activity to study brain function and treat neurological disorders.
How do homeobox genes work? | genetics | Homeobox genes encode transcription factors containing a homeodomain that binds DNA. They regulate embryonic development, body plan organization, and cell differentiation across diverse organisms (Hox genes in animals).
What is systems biology? | research | Systems biology studies complex interactions within biological systems using computational and mathematical modeling. It integrates data from genomics, transcriptomics, proteomics, and metabolomics to understand emergent properties.
Explain genetic correlation | research | Genetic correlation measures the extent to which genetic influences on one trait overlap with genetic influences on another trait. It helps understand pleiotropy (single genes affecting multiple traits) and shared biological pathways.
What is a chimera? | research | A chimera is an organism composed of cells from different zygotes. Natural chimeras can occur through fusion of embryos, while experimental chimeras are created in research to study development, stem cells, and organ transplantation.
How does DNA methylation work? | epigenetics | DNA methylation adds methyl groups to cytosine bases, typically at CpG dinucleotides, often silencing gene expression. It's essential for genomic imprinting, X-chromosome inactivation, and suppressing transposable elements.
What is genetic load? | evolution | Genetic load is the reduction in fitness of a population due to deleterious alleles compared to an ideal population with optimal genotypes. It includes mutation load, segregation load, and incompatibility load.
Explain fluorescence in situ hybridization | technology | FISH uses fluorescent probes that bind to complementary DNA sequences to visualize specific chromosome regions or genes. Applications include detecting chromosomal abnormalities, gene mapping, and identifying pathogens.
What is a genetic screen? | research | A genetic screen identifies genes involved in a biological process by systematically testing mutations for effects on a phenotype. Forward genetics starts with phenotype to find gene, reverse genetics starts with gene to find phenotype.
How do transcription factors work? | expression | Transcription factors are proteins that bind to specific DNA sequences to regulate transcription. They can activate or repress gene expression by recruiting RNA polymerase or chromatin modifiers to promoter/enhancer regions.
What is allopolyploidy? | evolution | Allopolyploidy occurs when hybridization between different species is followed by chromosome doubling, resulting in fertile polyploids with complete chromosome sets from both parents. Common in plants like wheat and cotton.
Explain single-cell sequencing | technology | Single-cell sequencing analyzes genomic, transcriptomic, or epigenomic information from individual cells, revealing cellular heterogeneity, developmental trajectories, and rare cell populations masked in bulk tissue analysis.
What is gene flow? | evolution | Gene flow is the transfer of genetic variation between populations through migration and interbreeding. It can introduce new alleles, increase genetic diversity, and counteract genetic drift and natural selection effects.
How do prions work? | disorders | Prions are misfolded proteins that can transmit their misfolded shape to normal variants of the same protein. They cause neurodegenerative diseases like Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease by accumulating in brain tissue.
What is a genetic bottleneck? | evolution | A genetic bottleneck occurs when a population's size is drastically reduced, leading to loss of genetic variation. The surviving population has non-representative allele frequencies that affect future generations.
Explain chromatin remodeling | epigenetics | Chromatin remodeling complexes use ATP to slide, eject, or restructure nucleosomes, making DNA more or less accessible to transcription machinery. This regulates gene expression without changing DNA sequence.
What is a synteny? | research | Synteny refers to conserved gene order on chromosomes between different species. Syntenic blocks indicate evolutionary relationships and help identify functionally important regions through comparative genomics.
How does RNA polymerase work? | processes | RNA polymerase synthesizes RNA from DNA template during transcription. Prokaryotes have one RNA polymerase, while eukaryotes have three: Pol I (rRNA), Pol II (mRNA, snRNA), Pol III (tRNA, 5S rRNA).
What is a restriction enzyme? | technology | Restriction enzymes cut DNA at specific recognition sequences, producing restriction fragments. They're molecular scissors used in DNA cloning, analysis, and recombinant DNA technology, originally discovered in bacteria.
Explain genetic compensation | genetics | Genetic compensation occurs when loss of gene function is compensated by altered expression of related genes or pathways, masking phenotypic effects. It explains why some gene knockouts show no obvious phenotype.
What is a ribosome? | structures | Ribosomes are molecular machines that synthesize proteins by translating mRNA sequences into amino acid chains. Composed of rRNA and proteins, they have large and small subunits that assemble during translation initiation.
How does nonsense-mediated decay work? | expression | Nonsense-mediated decay degrades mRNA containing premature termination codons, preventing production of truncated, potentially harmful proteins. It's a quality control mechanism that recognizes stop codons upstream of exon-exon junctions.
What is kin selection? | evolution | Kin selection explains altruistic behavior toward relatives by considering inclusive fitness. Genes promoting altruism can spread if they help relatives who share those genes, as described by Hamilton's rule.
Explain DNA fingerprinting | applications | DNA fingerprinting analyzes variable regions of DNA (like VNTRs or STRs) to identify individuals with high specificity. Used in forensics, paternity testing, and conservation genetics to distinguish between individuals.
What is a transposon? | genetics | Transposons (jumping genes) are DNA sequences that can change positions within the genome. They can cause mutations, alter gene expression, and contribute to genome evolution through cut-and-paste or copy-and-paste mechanisms.
How does the lac operon work? | expression | The lac operon in E. coli regulates lactose metabolism genes. In absence of lactose, a repressor binds operator, preventing transcription. Lactose binds repressor, inactivating it, allowing RNA polymerase to transcribe genes.
What is a genetic switch? | expression | Genetic switches are regulatory elements that turn genes on or off in response to signals. Examples include toggle switches (stable on/off states) and oscillators (rhythmic expression like circadian clocks).
Explain Muller's ratchet | evolution | Muller's ratchet describes how asexual populations accumulate deleterious mutations irreversibly over generations, as recombination cannot purge them. This gives advantage to sexual reproduction in removing harmful mutations.
What is a ribozyme? | structures | Ribozymes are RNA molecules with catalytic activity. They include self-splicing introns, RNase P (processes tRNA), and the ribosome's peptidyl transferase center, supporting the RNA world hypothesis.
How does imprinting work in disease? | epigenetics | Imprinting disorders occur when epigenetic marks at imprinted loci are disrupted. Examples include Prader-Willi syndrome (paternal deletion/maternal UPD) and Angelman syndrome (maternal deletion/paternal UPD).
What is a haplotype? | genetics | A haplotype is a set of DNA variants (alleles) inherited together on a single chromosome. Haplotype analysis helps track inheritance patterns, identify disease associations, and study population history.
Explain DNA barcoding | applications | DNA barcoding uses short, standardized gene regions (like COI in animals) to identify species. It aids biodiversity assessment, species discovery, and detection of illegal wildlife trade or food fraud.
What is a morphogen? | genetics | Morphogens are signaling molecules that form concentration gradients to specify different cell fates during development. Examples include Sonic hedgehog, BMPs, and Wnt proteins that pattern tissues and organs.
How does RNA-seq work? | technology | RNA-seq sequences cDNA from RNA samples to quantify gene expression across the transcriptome. It detects novel transcripts, alternative splicing, and gene fusions with higher sensitivity than microarrays.
What is a genetic network? | research | Genetic networks map interactions between genes, proteins, and other molecules in cellular processes. They reveal regulatory relationships, pathway connections, and system properties underlying phenotypes.
Explain frequency-dependent selection | evolution | Frequency-dependent selection occurs when a phenotype's fitness depends on its frequency relative to other phenotypes. Negative frequency-dependence maintains polymorphism (rare advantage), positive reduces it (common advantage).
What is a protoplast? | research | Protoplasts are plant cells with cell walls removed using enzymes. They're used for genetic transformation, somatic hybridization, and studying cell processes without wall interference in biotechnology research.
